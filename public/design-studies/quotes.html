Cross-slide · quotes (+ alternatives)

How we show "quotes"

Quotes appear on three slides — Reach, Engagement, and Still-Growing. Today each slide uses a different visual treatment, and that's a big part of the deck's incoherence.

But there's a deeper issue: the Reach and Still-Growing quotes are made up. "Marketing Lab, Brand Growth Report" and "CMO, B2C consumer brand" are stand-ins, not real attributions. So we're not just picking a quote style — we're deciding whether to frame these as quotes at all. This study explores both: four quote-style options (if we commit to the quote framing) and seven non-quote alternatives (where we reframe the content as an industry insight, a data point, a sticky-note aside, etc., owning the claim without inventing a source).

Trade-offs

AMagazine pull-quote Quote

Confident + editorial. Cite as eyebrow ABOVE the quote makes the source feel intentional, like an NYT byline.

  • Takes a lot of vertical space — crowds Reach + Engagement
  • Doesn't translate to creator comments (they need a face)
  • Strong on Still-Growing only

BVertical accent bar Quote

Unobtrusive but clearly "a quote." Gradient bar borrows from the brand accent.

  • Cleanest option for long quotes
  • Reads slightly Medium-y / blog-y
  • Cite line feels generic

CHanging serif Quote

Matches the deck's editorial tone. The decorative " at 64px earns its weight without screaming. Cite reuses the eyebrow style — quote attribution + section labels share one typographic family.

  • Same treatment scales to all three quote moods
  • Replaces Still-Growing's 140px " (too loud)
  • Avatar tucks into cite line on Engagement

DPhrase highlight Quote

Spotlights the most quotable bit using the brand gradient.

  • Strongest with short, punchy quotes
  • Falls apart on long quotes — unhighlighted text feels secondary
  • Doubling down on `gl-accent` makes the deck visually monochrome

EIndustry insight callout Not-a-quote

Owns the claim. "Industry insight" eyebrow positions the content as widely-held knowledge, not a quote from any specific person. Honest about being a made-up framing.

  • Soft card chrome separates it from the main slide body
  • Works on Reach + Still-Growing; awkward on Engagement (which has a real person)
  • Probably the safest non-quote option

FDid-you-know box Not-a-quote

Friendly, instructional. Frames the content as a fact the brand might not know.

  • Italic Fraunces header gives it editorial warmth
  • Reads like an explainer, not a celebration — clashes with the wrap-up's tone
  • Better on Still-Growing than Reach

GBig display claim Not-a-quote

No chrome, no attribution — just the words at big italic Fraunces. The most confident option. Trusts the message.

  • Tightest hierarchy: h2 → big claim, no card noise
  • Requires VERY good editing — short, punchy claims only
  • Could double as a poster moment for the slide

HStat card · data point Not-a-quote

Reframes the insight as a number with a source. Most credible-feeling — if we can find a real stat.

  • Requires real data we can cite (not just a vibe)
  • Visual treatment matches the deck's other stat moments
  • Replaces a quote with a fact — meatier

IInline lede · woven in Not-a-quote

The "quote" disappears entirely — its message becomes part of the slide's subtitle copy. Most minimal option.

  • Zero extra chrome — perfect for tight slides
  • Loses the "moment" feel — no callout to pause on
  • Italic Fraunces gives it just enough lift

JFrom Benable · first-party Not-a-quote

Brand voice. Benable owning the insight directly with the coral heart accent. Honest: we believe this, not some made-up CMO said this.

  • Reinforces Benable's POV across the deck
  • Could feel self-promotional if overused
  • Pairs naturally with Katie's postcard slide voice

KSticky note · hand-aside Not-a-quote

Playful, warm. Caveat hand font + yellow paper note + small tilt. Reads as a margin note from someone (Katie?) on a printout.

  • Echoes the Katie postcard slide
  • Could feel too cute for analytics slides
  • Best as a one-off accent, not a system applied everywhere

LWisdom ticker · minimal Not-a-quote

Tiny uppercase rule-style line under the headline. Almost no visual weight. Reads as a tagline or a "principle."

  • Easiest to fit in any slide
  • Almost ignorable — might fail to communicate
  • Strong only with VERY tight copy

MFootnote · bottom aside Not-a-quote

Pushes the insight to the bottom of the slide as small italic text. Reads as a sidebar / margin note.

  • Frees up the main slide body
  • Deprioritizes the message — risks being skipped
  • Could work as a consistent "fine print" pattern across slides

How to pick

Easy heuristic: if we're willing to commit to writing real, attributable industry quotes (or finding actual ones), go with C (Hanging serif) from the quote-style set. It's the most cohesive and scales to creator comments.

If we're not, the strongest non-quote options are E (Industry insight callout) for honesty, G (Big display claim) for confidence, or H (Stat card) if we can find a real number. E is probably the safest: it keeps the editorial feel, doesn't require finding real data, and doesn't pretend a quote is sourced when it isn't.

Mixing is also fine. Reach + Still-Growing could use E (industry insight, fabricated-content honesty), while Engagement keeps a quote treatment because its quote is real (a creator comment). The mix is intentional: real quotes get quoted, claims get framed as claims.
